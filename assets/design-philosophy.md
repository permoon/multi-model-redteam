# Schematic Editorial

## Manifesto

Information graphics treated as editorial typography. The diagram is
not an illustration of an idea — it *is* the idea, made visible
through the precise orchestration of typeface, scale, color
territory, and breathing room. Every line carries intent; every
margin is measured; every word earns its place by surviving a long
process of subtraction. What remains is a composition that reads as
slowly as a Helmut Schmid book spread and as quickly as a Vignelli
subway map — the same artifact serving both readings, depending on
the reader's pace.

The work belongs to the lineage of master information designers
who treated diagrams as serious visual literature: Otl Aicher's
pictograms for Munich '72, Massimo Vignelli's Unimark systems,
Edward Tufte's small multiples, the Nederlandse Bankbiljet's
classification grids. None of these works rely on decoration. Each
is the product of countless hours of refinement, the labor of
someone at the absolute top of their craft. The result feels
inevitable — as if no other arrangement could have communicated
the same thing.

## Visual expression

**Form and space.** A 1.4:1 horizontal canvas held in tension by
asymmetric distribution of weight. Negative space is treated as a
material with mass — empty regions are not absence but composition.
Generous gutters separate functional zones. The canvas breathes.

**Color as classification, not decoration.** A small intentional
palette: three warm-cool families assigned by function, each at low
saturation so that nothing screams. A single saturated accent
reserved for the focal moment — used once, with intent. Background
is not white but a warm near-white (a paper-stock hue) that gives
ink something to push against. Borders prefer hairline weights;
fills prefer the gentlest tonal lift.

**Scale and rhythm.** Typography in a hierarchy of three to four
sizes only. The largest weight reserved for one title moment,
treated almost as an architectural element. Subheads in a different
typeface to mark category change. Body labels in a calm grotesque.
Technical labels in a precise monospace, used as visual rhythm — a
pulse against the sans. Small italic marginalia thread through the
composition like a curator's notes in a catalogue.

**Composition and balance.** The piece is read top-to-bottom, but
weight is distributed asymmetrically left-to-right to avoid the
deadness of perfect symmetry. Connecting lines are drawn with
intent: stroke weights graduated, terminal markers minimal,
trajectories chosen for legibility over geometric purity. A
secondary grain runs throughout — a faint dot lattice or measured
rule lines — that signals "this is a designed instrument," not a
slide.

**Visual hierarchy.** First read: the title and overall flow.
Second read: the three classified zones, distinguished by color
territory. Third read: the focal accent, demanding attention
without shouting. Fourth read, for those who slow down: the
marginal annotations, the precise typesetting, the considered
spacing — the quiet evidence of a master at work. Each layer
rewards the reader who returns.

## Tone

Restrained. Authoritative. Editorial in the classical sense — the
voice of a serious magazine, not a marketing deck. Nothing apologises;
nothing performs. The work assumes the reader is intelligent and
treats them as such. It reads as the product of many revisions and
much subtraction, the kind of artifact that ages well because every
choice was deliberate.
